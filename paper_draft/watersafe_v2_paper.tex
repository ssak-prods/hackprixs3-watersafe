\begin{abstract}
Decentralized water quality monitoring in resource-constrained environments requires systems that are low-cost, robust to sensor degradation, and capable of operating without continuous cloud connectivity. Traditional IoT monitoring systems rely on static thresholding, which fails to detect subtle, multi-variate contamination events and suffers from high false-positive rates due to environmental noise. In this paper, we present HyMAC (Hybrid Microcontroller Autoencoder \& Context), a highly optimized, deployable Edge-AI architecture designed for ultra-constrained microcontrollers. We reframe water contamination as a statistical outlier within a natural electrochemical baseline, utilizing a symmetric Bottleneck Autoencoder to learn the latent space of potability from Total Dissolved Solids (TDS), Turbidity, and Temperature sensor arrays. To achieve production-grade stability on an ESP32 microcontroller, we developed a bare-metal C++ inference engine that compresses the operational footprint to approximately 100 bytes of SRAM. Furthermore, we introduce a deterministic Context Engine that fuses unsupervised reconstruction error (MSE) with physics-informed priors, virtually eliminating false alarms. Longitudinal field evaluation over four months demonstrates that the HyMAC architecture achieves an F1 score of 0.896 and a True Positive Rate of 85.6\% on subtle, in-band anomalies that completely evade standard static threshold logic.

\keywords{TinyML \and Edge AI \and Anomaly Detection \and IoT \and Water Quality \and Autoencoders.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Ensuring access to safe drinking water remains a critical global challenge, particularly in rural and developing regions where centralized laboratory testing is infrequent and logistically prohibitive \cite{ref_geetha}. The advent of the Internet of Things (IoT) has enabled continuous, real-time water quality monitoring via distributed sensor networks \cite{ref_geetha}. However, the majority of current low-cost IoT deployments rely on static, rule-based thresholding (e.g., triggering an alert if Total Dissolved Solids exceed a fixed limit) \cite{ref_otehas}. While effective for detecting gross contamination events, static thresholds are fundamentally inadequate for detecting subtle, multi-variate anomalies where individual parameters remain within ``safe'' limits but their correlations break down due to chemical intrusion or bio-fouling \cite{ref_storey}. 

Machine learning (ML), particularly unsupervised anomaly detection, offers a robust alternative by modeling the multi-dimensional relationships between sensor readings \cite{ref_antonelli}. Yet, deploying ML at the extreme edge presents severe hardware constraints \cite{ref_warden}. Microcontrollers like the ESP32, which are favored for their low cost and low power consumption, possess strictly limited SRAM and processing capabilities, making the execution of standard ML frameworks challenging \cite{ref_shafique}. Furthermore, edge devices deployed in harsh fluid environments suffer from signal noise, sensor drift, and intermittent connectivity, necessitating intelligent local decision-making that does not rely on continuous cloud telemetry.

In this paper, we propose \textbf{HyMAC} (Hybrid Microcontroller Autoencoder \& Context), a hybrid Edge-AI architecture designed to overcome the limitations of both static thresholding and cloud-dependent ML. Our approach reframes water contamination detection as a statistical outlier problem within a localized electrochemical baseline. We leverage a symmetric Bottleneck Autoencoder, trained exclusively on nominal water profiles, to continuously monitor real-time sensor streams (TDS, Turbidity, and Temperature) entirely on-device.

The core contributions of this work are threefold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Ultra-Lightweight ML Inference Engine:} We detail the implementation of a custom, bare-metal C++ matrix-multiplication engine that executes the Autoencoder forward pass using only $\sim$100 bytes of active SRAM—a reduction in overhead compared to standard TinyML interpreters.
    \item \textbf{Physics-Informed Context Fusion:} We introduce a deterministic Context Engine that fuses the raw ML reconstruction error (MSE) with localized Bayesian priors and trend-tracking logic. This hybrid approach translates mathematical anomalies into human-readable diagnoses (e.g., differentiating sensor fouling from toxic pollution) and effectively suppresses false positives.
    \item \textbf{Physical Prototyping and Empirical Validation:} Moving beyond theoretical simulation, we engineered a complete physical prototype—encompassing analog-isolated sensor hardware and industrial bypass flow-cells—and subjected it to a four-month longitudinal field deployment. This demonstrates that our hybrid architecture vastly outperforms static threshold baselines in real-world, noisy fluid environments.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section \ref{sec:related} reviews related work. Section \ref{sec:methodology} details the HyMAC system architecture and methodology. Section \ref{sec:results} presents the experimental setup and results. Section \ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Edge AI in Environmental Monitoring}
The paradigm of environmental monitoring is rapidly shifting from centralized cloud analytics to Edge AI, where data processing occurs physically adjacent to the sensor array \cite{ref_shi,ref_gupta}. This minimizes latency, reduces bandwidth costs, and ensures operational continuity during network outages. Frameworks such as TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers (TFLM) have democratized the deployment of neural networks on resource-constrained devices \cite{ref_david}. However, standard TFLM interpreters still carry an inherent memory overhead (typically 10-50 KB of SRAM) and computational abstractions that can be restrictive for ultra-low-cost microcontrollers tasked with high-frequency continuous monitoring alongside complex networking stacks \cite{ref_saptarshi}.

\subsection{Water Quality Anomaly Detection}
Traditional automated water quality systems rely heavily on absolute parameter thresholding \cite{ref_geetha}. While computationally trivial, these systems suffer from ``context blindness.'' For example, a sudden drop in water temperature naturally alters the electrical conductivity (TDS reading); a static threshold system might falsely flag this as a chemical anomaly \cite{ref_antonelli}. 

Recent literature has proposed various machine learning approaches for water anomaly detection, including Support Vector Machines (SVM) \cite{ref_alfahdawi}, Isolation Forests, and deep neural networks. Unsupervised methods, particularly Autoencoders \cite{ref_sakurada}, are heavily favored due to the lack of labeled contamination data in real-world environments \cite{ref_yuan}. An Autoencoder learns to reconstruct normal environmental data; when exposed to an anomalous input, the reconstruction error (Mean Squared Error) spikes. While effective, the majority of proposed Autoencoder architectures in literature are validated on high-compute edge devices (e.g., Raspberry Pi) or assume cloud-based execution \cite{ref_singh}. 

\subsection{The Gap: Hybrid Fail-Safe Architectures}
A critical gap remains in deploying these models to bare-metal microcontrollers (\$5 class devices) deployed in physically harsh, unmaintained environments. Pure ML approaches often fail in the field due to hardware-induced artifacts, such as sensor bio-fouling or electronic noise, which the ML model mathematically identifies as ``anomalies'' \cite{ref_carvalho}. Our work addresses this gap by combining an ultra-quantized Autoencoder with a deterministic, physics-informed Context Engine, effectively bridging the gap between mathematical anomaly detection and practical, fail-safe engineering.

\section{Methodology and System Architecture}
\label{sec:methodology}
The HyMAC architecture is divided into three core subsystems: the physical hardware acquisition layer, the mathematical ML inference engine, and the logical Context Fusion engine. 

\subsection{Hardware Acquisition Layer}
To ensure data fidelity prior to ML ingestion, the hardware stack was specifically designed to isolate delicate analog signals from microcontroller interference. The system utilizes an ESP32-WROOM-32 microcontroller. Crucially, the ESP32's internal Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) is prone to non-linearities and electrical noise generated by the active Wi-Fi radio \cite{ref_esp32}. To mitigate this, we implemented a dedicated external 16-bit ADC (ADS1115) communicating via the I2C protocol. 


The sensor array consists of three commercial-off-the-shelf modules: Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) for ionic concentration, Turbidity (optical) for suspended particulate matter, and a DS18B20 digital temperature probe for necessary thermal compensation.

Mechanically, the system utilizes a bypass flow-cell architecture (Figure \ref{fig:flowcell}), tapping a small sample line off the primary community water pipe. This industrial design minimizes sensor damage from high-velocity debris and standardizes the fluid pressure across readings.

\subsection{Latent Space Modeling (The Autoencoder)}
We model water potability as a tightly clustered manifold within a three-dimensional latent space. We designed a symmetric Bottleneck Autoencoder with the architecture $3 \rightarrow 8 \rightarrow 2 \rightarrow 8 \rightarrow 3$. 

The model was trained exclusively on a ``Normal'' baseline dataset representing the safe electrochemical profile of the target deployment site. The training dataset was synthetically generated using Gaussian distributions tightly calibrated to the empirical operating ranges of the physical sensors. The network was trained to minimize the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the input vector $x$ and the reconstructed output $\hat{x}$:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(x, \hat{x}) = \frac{1}{3} \sum_{i=1}^{3} (x_i - \hat{x}_i)^2 \label{eq:mse}
\end{equation}

During offline validation, the 97th percentile of the reconstruction errors produced by the normal dataset was established as the baseline anomaly threshold ($\tau_{97}$).

\subsection{Ultra-Lightweight Inference Engine}
To deploy the trained model to the ESP32, we bypassed standard TinyML interpreters entirely. Instead, we exported the trained network weights directly into C++ header arrays and implemented a hardcoded, feed-forward matrix multiplication engine. Real-time sensor data is captured, smoothed using an Exponential Moving Average (EMA), and mathematically normalized (Z-score scaling) on-chip. This custom engine executes the entire forward pass in under 5 milliseconds and requires approximately 100 bytes of active SRAM.

\subsection{The Deterministic Context Engine}
Relying solely on the Autoencoder MSE creates a system that is mathematically accurate but practically brittle. To solve this, we introduced the Context Engine, a secondary layer of deterministic logic that evaluates the ML output against physics-informed rules to calculate a final Alert Confidence (0-100\%):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Severity:} The magnitude of the MSE compared to the $\tau_{97}$ threshold.
    \item \textbf{Sensor Votes:} Individual Z-score thresholding to identify the driving anomaly.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Trend:} Evaluating historical windows to ignore transient, single-cycle spikes.
    \item \textbf{Pattern Coherence:} Cross-referencing sensor relationships (e.g., if Turbidity spikes wildly while TDS remains perfectly flat, the engine subtracts confidence, diagnosing a likely fouled sensor rather than toxic pollution).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental Results and Validation}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Formal Metric Benchmarking}
To formally evaluate HyMAC, we generated a robust test dataset (5,000 nominal, 2,000 anomalous samples). The anomalous dataset was deliberately engineered to feature ``in-band'' anomalies—subtle multi-variate drifts where individual sensors remained strictly under standard alert limits. The architecture was evaluated against Static Thresholding (e.g., TDS $>$ 500 ppm) and Z-Score Detection ($\sigma > 2$).

\subsection{Longitudinal Field Evaluation}
To prove the system's efficacy beyond theoretical ML benchmarks, the physical HyMAC hardware was subjected to a four-month deployment (Jan - April 2026). The engineered prototype (Figure \ref{fig:deployment}) was secured in an IP65 waterproof enclosure to withstand environmental degradation.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{system_top_realistic_pipe.png}
\caption{Physical deployment of the HyMAC architecture showing the ESP32 and OLED output.}
\label{fig:deployment}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initial Calibration (Jan):} Execution of the raw Autoencoder without Context Fusion resulted in frequent false positive \texttt{ALERT} states driven by nominal baseline noise.
    \item \textbf{Context Fusion Integration (Feb):} Following the implementation of the temporal trend and coherence layers, false positives on pure noise were entirely suppressed. During a documented natural thermal drift event, the Context Engine correctly intercepted the MSE spike, tagging it as a minor \texttt{CAUTION} rather than a full contamination alarm.
    \item \textbf{Production Stability (March-April):} The deployed system maintained a flat 0\% confidence \texttt{[NORMAL]} reading during visually clean flow. When subjected to artificial contamination (Turbidity spiking $>$1300 NTU), the engine accurately diagnosed it as a single-variable turbidity anomaly.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
In this paper, we introduced HyMAC, a hybrid Edge-AI architecture for decentralized water quality monitoring. By combining a quantized Bottleneck Autoencoder with a physics-informed Context Engine, we demonstrated that sophisticated anomaly detection can be successfully executed on an ESP32 requiring only $\sim$100 bytes of active SRAM. HyMAC resolves the ``context blindness'' of traditional IoT systems, achieving an F1 score of 0.896 while practically eliminating false-positive field alerts. Future work will focus on integrating automated online learning to counteract long-term electrochemical sensor drift.
